10 Tips to Improve Your Artist Workflow with RefBoard

Published on January 14, 2024 · 6 min read · Productivity

As digital artists, our workflow can make or break our productivity. A well-organized reference system isn't just about keeping files tidy—it's about freeing your mind to focus on creativity rather than file management. After working with hundreds of professional artists, we've identified the workflows that separate hobbyists from pros. Here are 10 proven tips to transform how you use RefBoard and supercharge your creative process.

1 Create Project-Specific Boards

Instead of dumping all your references into one massive board, create separate boards for each project. This prevents visual overwhelm and keeps your mind focused. For example, if you're working on a fantasy character, create dedicated boards for "Main Character Design," "Costume References," "Color Palette," and "Environment Mood." This compartmentalization mirrors how professional studios organize their concept art pipelines.

2 Use the Rule of Threes for Grouping

When organizing references, group related images in sets of three. This psychological principle (the" rule of three") helps your brain process and compare variations more effectively. For character design, you might group three different armor styles, three lighting scenarios, or three pose variations. This makes decision-making faster and more confident than comparing dozens of disparate references.

3 Build Mood Before Details

Start each project by creating a mood board with emotional, atmospheric references before diving into technical details. Spend your first hour gathering images that capture the *feeling* you want to convey—colors, lighting, composition, mood. Only after this foundation is solid should you add specific technical references like anatomy charts or costume details. This top-down approach ensures your work has emotional coherence, not just technical accuracy.

4 Implement a Color-Coded Group System

RefBoard allows you to customize group border colors. Use this feature strategically: assign blue borders to "must use" references, yellow to" maybe references," and green to "inspiration only." This visual triage system helps you prioritize quickly when you're deep in the creative zone and don't want to break flow to make decisions.

5 Schedule Weekly Reference Reviews

Set aside 30 minutes each week to review and cull your reference boards. As projects evolve, early references often become irrelevant or even misleading. Delete outdated references ruthlessly—a leaner, more focused board beats a cluttered comprehensive one every time. Professional concept artists at studios like Blizzard and Riot Games follow this practice religiously.

6 Create a Personal Reference Library

Beyond project-specific boards, maintain a personal library board organized by categories: "Lighting," "Materials," "Anatomy," "Composition," etc. When you encounter an excellent reference online, immediately add it to your library. Over time, this becomes an invaluable resource that you can pull from for any project. Think of it as building your own visual encyclopedia.

7 Use Spatial Organization Intentionally

RefBoard's infinite canvas isn't just for dumping images randomly. Use spatial positioning to mirror your creative process. Place inspiration references at the top, technical references in the middle, and work-in-progress exports at the bottom. Or organize left-to-right by project stage: concept → rough → refined → final. This spatial memory helps you navigate large boards intuitively.

8 Em brace the 80/20 Rule

Not all references are created equal. Follow the Pareto Principle: 80% of your creative decisions will come from 20% of your references. Identify your "hero references"—the 3-5 images that most perfectly capture your vision—and position them prominently on your board. Refer to these constantly while letting the supporting references fade into the periphery. This prevents decision paralysis from too many options.

9 Collaborate with Intention

When sharing boards with team members or clients, create a separate "Presentation" board rather than sharing your messy working board. Export your hero references and key decisions to this clean board. This professional presentation not only looks better but also guides the conversation more productively. Your working board can stay chaotic—that's where creativity happens.

10 Build Reference-Gathering Into Your Routine

The most successful artists don't wait until they start a project to gather references. Make reference collection a daily habit: spend 10-15 minutes each morning browsing art sites like ArtStation, Pinterest, or Instagram, and immediately add compelling images to RefBoard. When a project starts, you'll already have a rich library to draw from instead of scrambling to find references mid-project.

Implementing These Tips

Don't try to implement all 10 tips at once—that's a recipe for overwhelm. Start with just one or two that resonate most with your current workflow challenges. Give each tip at least two weeks to become a habit before adding another. The compounding effect of even a few optimizations can dramatically improve your productivity over time.

The Real Secret: Consistency Over Perfection

The most important workflow tip isn't on this list because it's too fundamental: consistency beats perfection. A simple system you use every day is infinitely more valuable than a perfect system you abandon after a week. Start with basic organization, use RefBoard regularly, and let your workflow evolve naturally. The best workflow is the one that serves your creative process, not someone else's ideal.

RefBoard is designed to adapt to your unique creative process, not force you into a rigid structure. Use these tips as starting points, then customize them to fit how you actually work. The goal isn't to follow rules—it's to remove friction from your creative process so you can focus on making great art.
